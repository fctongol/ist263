Retirement Planning

401(k)

What is a 401(k)? |

A retirement savings and investing plan that employers usually offer. Unless you’re balancing a full-time job with benefits along with your classes, this plan is something just to consider once you graduate and are looking for your first jobs.

The amount of money put into your 401k is automatically taken from your paychecks and are invested in a pool of mutual funds of your choosing.

Why do a 401(k) rather than investing yourself? |

If you’re fortunate, your employer offers to match a portion of how much you put into your 401(k) in which case, you’re getting more put into your investment without hurting your paycheck.

This can also be considered as a passive form of investing as your employers are handling most of the work while you simply just have to provide and choose.

401(k) contributions can lower your income taxes. Meaning the amount of your income that you contributed to a 401(k) is subtracted from your total income, and that amount is what’s taxed.

Social Security

Social security benefits covers multiple areas, and one of those is retirement. Employees who worked in social security covered employment for a sufficient number of years are eligible for retirement benefits by age 62. You usually must have worked a total of at least 10 years at a nongovernmental jobs under these conditions. This is due to the need to accumulate 40 credits that are earned by earning a minimum amount of money over a course of a year and it takes most people a decade to add up to 40. This minimum amount is adjusted over time to keep up with inflation.

This retirement benefit is in the form of a check that is given to you when you decide to begin receiving benefits any time after you’re 62 years old. However, the amount of your benefits are permanent reduced by a percentage if you claim them before you reach the full retirement age which is between 66 and 67.

The monthly retirement benefit check varies from person to person depending on when you decided to claim them and if your earnings were above average incomes.

For example, those who retired in 2019 at their full retirement age got a maximum monthly benefit of $2,861 per month -- or $34,332 for the whole year. For those who waited until age 70 to start collecting benefits, the monthly maximum was $3,770, or $45,240 annually, while those who started at age 62 would have received a maximum of $2,209 per month, or $26,508 for the year

Roth/Traditional IRA

IRA stands for individual retirement account and there are two types you can contribute to. They’re investment accounts that similarly work to a 401(k) meant for saving for retirement in a tax-advantaged way.

Roth IRA |

A Roth IRA allows you to make after-tax contributions. They’re best for individuals who expect to be in a higher tax bracket when they start withdrawing their earnings from it.

  • No required minimum distribution rules and are tax and penalty free
  • No tax deductions for contributing
  • Ability to contribute is phased out at higher incomes
  • Unless you meet exceptions, early withdrawals of contributions and earnings are taxed and subject to a 10% penalty

Traditional IRA |

A Traditional IRA allows you to make pre-tax contributions. They’re best for individuals who expect to be in the same or lower tax-bracket when they start withdrawing their earnings from it.

  • Require you to start taking required minimum distributions at age 72
  • Contributions made reduce your taxable income
  • Withdrawals are taxable as ordinary income
  • Unless you meet exceptions, early withdrawals of contributions and earnings are taxed and subject to a 10% penalty
