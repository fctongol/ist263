Taxes

Types of Taxes


Taxes. No one wants them, but they’ll always exist. As college students, there are generally four taxes you’ll come across before you graduate.

| Consumption Tax

This tax is something we’re all too familiar with the moment we started paying for our own things. The consumption tax we come across often are sales tax which is a percentage of the price of what you’re purchasing in addition to its original cost. It’s something we’re used to, but need to keep in mind especially when making large purchases that add up to way more than what it’s advertised.

| Payroll Tax

This tax is a percentage of an employee’s pay that is paid to the government by their employer on the employee’s behalf. This tax is dedicated to fund government programs such as Medicare and Social Security in which you reap the benefits later in your life.

| Federal Income Tax

This tax is a percentage of an employee’s pay that is determined by their tax bracket or how much they earned in the U.S. Income taxes are a source of revenue for governments. Although most people would like to keep their entire income, this tax benefits more than just an individual as its used to fund public services, pay government obligation, and provide goods for citizens.

| State Income Tax

This tax is determined by your state of residence and some states have no state income tax at all (Alask, Florida, Nevada, South Dakota, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming.) Like the Federal Income Tax, this money is used to fund services like transportation, corrections, health benefits for public employees, environmental projects, state police, etc.

| Bonus For Investors: Capital Gains Tax

If you decide to be a student investor, you also need to realize there are Capital Gains taxes you need to consider and file. Capital Gains taxes are taxes on the profits you earn from your stock investments. The tax rate on your capital gains depends on how long you held onto your shares before selling. Holding for more than one year result in 0%, 15% or 20% taxes, but holding for less than a year usually correspond to ordinary income tax rates.

Tax Credits & Deductions

American Opportunity Credit |

Claim a credit of up to $2,500 for the first four years of college for tuition and fees, books, supplies, and equipment. It can be claimed for each student in the family, but there are adjusted gross income limits that apply.

| Tuition and Fees Deduction

Students who paid for tuition, books, supplies, etc. for a degree program they were enrolled in 2020 could be eligible to reduce taxable income by up to $4,000. This can’t be claimed with any other education-related federal tax credits. And once again there are adjusted gross income limits that apply.
